# The Parable of the Dough

---

A teacher sat among those who sought understanding, and one asked him:

*"Why must we suffer? Why were we not simply made whole?"*

The teacher smiled and said:

---

**"Listen, then.**

In the beginning, God breathed a single, living breath — and from it shaped a soul, whole and luminous, as a baker shapes one great loaf from the finest flour. He set it in a garden and called it Adam, and from Adam drew Eve, and they were one dough, one substance, unspoiled.

But the Enemy is cunning, and he does not poison the whole loaf. He touches only the edge. A small corruption — a single fold of the dough turned inward upon itself, choosing *its own* savor over the Baker's.

And what was this turning? Consider: they had walked naked before God and before each other and felt no shame. But after the corruption, the first thing they knew was their nakedness, and the first thing they reached for was covering. What had been pure self-giving now carried within it the seed of taking. They had not yet borne children. The corruption preceded the multiplication.

Now — the teacher leaned forward — what does a wise baker do when a portion of his dough is touched by rot? Does he throw away the whole batch? No. Not if the greater part is still good. He tears the dough into pieces. He separates. He examines each piece. He sets aside what is clean and discards what is not.

And so it was. Adam and Eve bore children, and in the bearing, the one soul breathed by God was shared — divided — as a single flame lights many candles without diminishing, yet each candle now carries its own portion of light *and* its own portion of smoke. Each child received a share of the original dough, and with it, some measure of the corruption at its edge.

This is what the Church calls *original sin* — not a punishment, but a *condition*. As if every piece torn from the loaf still carried, somewhere in its folds, a trace of what had turned.

But hear this: not every piece was torn the same.

Some pieces came from near the center of the loaf, where the dough was mostly clean. Others were torn from nearer the edge, where the corruption had worked deeper. This is why one man is born with a gentle spirit and another with a rage he did not choose. Why one woman finds faith as easy as breathing and another must wrestle God like Jacob through every night of her life. It is not that God loved one more than the other. It is that each piece carries a different portion of the original turning, and each must be worked differently to be made clean.

And this — *this* — is why you carry your cross.

Have you ever watched a baker knead dough? He does not leave it to sit. He presses it. He folds it and pushes it flat and folds it again. He strikes it against the table. If the dough could speak, it would say: *Why do you hurt me? I was resting. I was comfortable.* And the baker would answer: *Because there is something trapped inside you that will not come out any other way.*

Suffering is the kneading.

Every cross you carry — the illness that will not lift, the grief that hollows you, the weakness you fight and fight and cannot conquer, the injustice you did not earn — each one is the Baker's hands pressing into *your* piece of dough, working at the fold where the corruption hides. Not because He is cruel. Because the rot is *deep*, and gentleness alone will not reach it.

This is why the Lord said *'Take up your cross and follow me.'* Because each piece of dough has its own hidden fold, and only the pressure applied to *that* fold, in *that* place, will open it.

The man born with rage — his cross is the long labor of patience, and every time he restrains his hand, the fold loosens a little. The woman who wrestles with doubt — her cross is the wrestling itself, and every night she chooses to hold on, the dough yields. The child born into suffering she did nothing to deserve — her cross is the oldest mystery, and the Baker weeps as He kneads, but He does not stop, because He sees what she cannot: the bread she will become.

Generation after generation, the Baker worked His dough. Not in anger. In patience. Sifting. Kneading. Tearing and examining. The way a woman sifts flour through a fine mesh — not to destroy the flour, but to find what is pure.

And did not the Lord Himself teach this? *'Let both grow together until the harvest. At that time I will tell the harvesters: first collect the weeds and tie them in bundles to be burned; then gather the wheat and bring it into my barn.'*

The sifting took generations. Abraham. Isaac. Jacob. David. A lineage not of perfect men — for there were none — but of a dough being worked, kneaded, refined. And at last, after all the patient tearing and sifting, there came a young woman in whom the flour was clean. The Church knows her as Mary — conceived without the stain, a piece of the original dough in which no trace of the corruption remained. Pure flour, at last, after all the sifting.

Now pause and consider something.

In the garden there stood two trees. Two. Not one. The Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil, and the Tree of Life. Through the first tree, the corruption entered — it was the vessel through which the turning came into the dough. But the second tree stood untouched. Adam and Eve were cast out before they could reach it.

Mary is the Tree of Life.

As the first tree was the vessel through which sin entered the world, so Mary is the vessel through which salvation entered. One tree bore fruit that corrupted; the other bore fruit that saves. And just as no one forced Adam's hand toward the first tree — he chose — so no one forced Mary's *yes*. She chose. The vessel was not taken. It was offered.

But — and hear this carefully — clean flour alone does not feed the hungry. It must be made into bread.

And so God did not merely celebrate the clean flour. The same God who had first breathed the dough into being now placed His own life within it. Not as more flour — for flour, however pure, is still only dust. He entered as living water. For what is flour without water? It cannot hold together. It cannot become bread. It remains scattered powder on the table. The Son was not merely a clean piece of Adam's soul. He was something the dough had never contained: the Baker Himself, come down into His own creation — the living water that binds the flour into bread.

Why?

Because the corruption could not be merely removed. It had to be *overcome*. You cannot heal a sickness simply by finding one person who is not sick. You need a cure — something that enters the sickness and defeats it from within.

This is the mystery of the Cross.

Think of the dough. All those pieces, scattered across the ages — billions of fragments of the one breath God breathed into Adam — each carrying its portion of turning, its inheritance of self-choosing. How could they be gathered back?

Imagine the sun. Its rays pour outward — millions upon millions of beams scattering into the cold. Each ray is real light, true light, *from* the sun. But each ray, the farther it travels, grows colder, dimmer, more alone. It does not stop being light. But it has traveled so far from its source that it has nearly forgotten what warmth is.

Christ on the Cross is the moment the sun *calls its rays home*.

Not by force. By love. He stretches out and says: *I am the source you forgot. I am the warmth you have been seeking in every wrong hearth. Come back.*

His death did not destroy the scattered pieces. It *reversed their direction*. Every fragment of the original dough, every soul torn and divided since Adam, now has a path back to the whole.

But a path must be walked.

And so the Baker left instructions.

First: *Baptism*. This is the washing of the flour. The piece of dough, still carrying its inherited corruption, is plunged into water and the stain is dissolved. Not the dough — the stain. The soul is not destroyed and remade. It is *cleaned*. What was folded inward upon itself is opened again toward God. The ray of light is turned back toward the sun.

But we live in a dusty world, and clean flour does not stay clean on its own. We touch what we should not touch. We fold inward again. The old habit of self-choosing reasserts itself, not because the washing failed, but because the Baker set the clean dough back upon the same table where the rot once was.

And so: *Confession*. Return to the water, again and again. Not because God is impatient, but because He is patient — infinitely, absurdly patient. He will wash the same piece of dough seventy times seven times and never once say it is not worth the effort.

And finally: *the Eucharist*. And here you must listen most carefully, for this is the deepest mystery of all.

Remember: Christ was born of Mary — the one piece of dough the sifting had made perfectly clean, the Tree of Life made flesh. And into that clean dough, God Himself entered as living water. So what was Christ's body? It was the purest dough that had ever existed — the original flour of Adam with none of the corruption, bound together by the life of God Himself.

Now recall what He said at the Last Supper. He did not say *'This represents my body.'* He did not say *'This reminds you of me.'* He took the bread — *dough*, baked and broken — and said: *'This IS my body.'*

Do you see?

The Baker took the one perfect piece of dough — the piece that the whole history of sifting and kneading had been laboring toward — and He *broke it*. He broke it the way the original loaf had been broken in the bearing of children, but this time not to sift out corruption. This time to *distribute the cure*.

Every fragment of that broken bread is pure dough. When you consume it, you are not merely remembering the Baker. You are taking the purest dough into your own corrupted piece. The clean enters the unclean — not to be contaminated by it, but to *convert* it. Slowly. From the inside.

This is why the Eucharist is not a symbol. It is the very thing the parable has been pointing to all along.

The original dough was torn apart because corruption demanded separation. The body of Christ is torn apart because love demands *reunion*. What sin scattered, the Eucharist gathers. Piece by piece. Soul by soul. Every time you receive that bread, the pure dough mixes with your own, and the fold where your corruption hides is worked open — not by your effort alone, but by the living presence of the Baker *inside your own substance*.

This is why you must return, again and again. Not because one communion was not enough, but because the kneading is not finished. You are still on the table. The Baker is still working. And every time you receive the bread, His hands reach deeper into the fold.

And so, child — the teacher's voice grew quiet — when you ask *'Why must I suffer?'* — understand what you are.

You are a piece of the original dough. You carry within you a breath that God Himself exhaled. You also carry, in the hidden folds of your nature, a turning that is older than your name. You did not choose it. But it is yours. And the cross you bear — *yours*, not another's — is the exact pressure needed to open *your* fold, in *your* place, where *your* corruption hides.

Some pieces require more kneading than others. Not because they are less loved, but because their fold is deeper. And the Baker, who knows every crease in every piece, applies to each one only what it needs — no more, no less.

And everything — *everything* — the long lineage of generations, the sifting, the patient tearing, the coming of Mary, the Incarnation, the Cross, the water, the confession, and the bread that is His very body given back to you — all of it is the Baker's answer to one small corruption on the edge of one loaf, in one garden, at the beginning of the world.

He did not discard the dough.

He is making bread."

---

*A note on what you have just read:*

*This parable was not received as scripture, nor does it claim to be. It began as a handful of thoughts — a few high-level intuitions about dough, corruption, and the sifting of souls — offered by a person who cannot say with certainty whether they arose from his own mind or were placed there by something greater.*

*From those seeds, an artificial mind — trained on theology, centuries of human reflection, and the bible — expanded and shaped the telling. Whether that makes it less true or more, the reader must judge. But consider: if God is the source of all making, then every mind that fashions anything — organic or artificial — is, at least indirectly, His creation.*

*Nothing here is intended to contradict what the Church teaches. Where it resonates with truth, give glory to God. Where it falls short, blame the imperfect dough.*
